Justifying consequences of Mertens' Theorem

With reference to earlier posts about Mertens' Theorem and descent in prime sieves, I attempt to explain why an intuitive approximation to the prime counting function significantly overestimates the density of primes below a given bound.

Descent in prime sieves

I use descent to represent a function analogous to the prime counting function as the sum of floored terms, then use Taylor and Fourier series to express it analytically. I intend to expand the domain to two dimensions, and analyse the resulting graph in the context of prime k-tuples.

The behaviour of overlapping modular functions

I investigate the periodic, one-to-one nature of functions reliant on multiple modular congruencies. I then apply the findings to determine a lower bound for the functions τ(N) and υ(N) from my Mertens' Theorem post.

The significance of Mertens' Theorem

I define Mertens' Theorem and explain how it contradicts intuition around prime sieves. I conjecture that its surprising effect accumulates over many infintessimal differences, presented as a hypothesis concerning a local function υ(N). I then relate Mertens' theorem back to the prime k-tuple conjecture.

What is the prime k-tuple conjecture?

A definition of Hardy and Littlewood's prime k-tuple conjecture, which is a general case of the more famous Twin Prime Conjecture. The prime k-tuple conjecture was the motivation behind a lot of the mathematics on this blog.
