The significance of Mertens' Theorem

Mertens' third theorem states that \[\lim_{N\to\infty}\ln{N}\prod_{p'=2}^{N}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)=e^{-\gamma},\] and by the Prime Number Theorem, the density of primes below \(N\), denoted \(d(N)\), has asymptotic behaviour \[d(N)\sim\frac{1}{\ln{N}};\] then by the transitive property, it is known that \[d(N)\sim e^{\gamma}\prod_{p'=2}^{N}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right).\]

Consider the natural numbers from \(1\) to \(N\) inclusive. Removing all multiples of each prime \(p\) for which \(2 \le p\le \sqrt{N}\) leaves only primes remaining. Note that \(1\) is considered prime. Let \(R_p(N)\) return the ratio \[ \frac{\mid\{ n \text{ s.t. } n\le N \land \forall k'\le p, k\not\mid n\}\mid}{\mid\{n\text{ s.t. } n\le N \land \forall k' < p, k\not\mid n\}\mid}; \] then the product \(\prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}R_p(N)\) is less than \(d(N)\). This algorithm removes primes below \(\sqrt{N}\), as they are multiples of themselves, but leaves all other primes below \(N\). Then the prime number theorem implies that \(\pi(\sqrt{N})=o(\pi(N))\), so that \[ \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}} R_p(N) \sim d(N). \]

It seems reasonable to estimate that as \(N\to\infty\), \[ \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}R_p(N)\sim \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right); \] this is heuristically justified by the fact that for distinct primes \(k_1\) and \(k_2\), \(k_1\not\mid n\) and \(k_2\not\mid n\) are independent events with respective probabilities of approximately \(1-\frac{1}{k_1}\) and \(1-\frac{1}{k_2}\). The trivial corollary of Mertens' third theorem deduced below contradicts this: \[\lim_{N\to\infty}\ln{\sqrt{N}}\prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)=e^{-\gamma}\]

\[\implies \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right) \sim \frac{e^{-\gamma}}{\ln{\sqrt{N}}}\]

\[\implies \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right) \sim \frac{2e^{-\gamma}}{\ln{N}} \sim 2e^{-\gamma}d(N)\]

\[\therefore d(N) \sim \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}} R_p(N) \sim \frac{e^{\gamma}}{2} \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right).\] It is worth noting that \(\frac{1}{2}e^{\gamma}\) is less than \(1\), being approximately \(0.890522...\); this implies that \(\prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)\) is an overestimation of the density of primes below \(N\).

Let \(\tau\) and \(\upsilon\) be the greatest functions on \(N\) below \(\sqrt{N}\) that satisfy, respectively: \[ \prod_{p'=2}^{\tau(N)}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)\sim\prod_{p'=2}^{\tau(N)}R_p(N) \]

\[ \forall p'\le\upsilon(N), 1-\frac{1}{p} \sim R_p(N). \] While Mertens' theorem implies \(\tau(N) < \sqrt{N}\), it is still possible that \(\upsilon(N)=\sqrt{N}\). For example, suppose that for each \(p'\le\sqrt{N}\), \(R_p(N)\sim \exp\left({\frac{\gamma}{\pi(\sqrt{N})}}\right) \left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)\). Then, since \(\exp\left({\frac{\gamma}{\pi(\sqrt{N})}}\right)\to 1\), \(1-\frac{1}{p} \sim R_p(N)\) for all \(p'\le\sqrt{N}\) and consequently \(\upsilon(N)=\sqrt{N}\). Note that if \(\upsilon(N)=\sqrt{N}\), then \(\tau(N)<\sqrt{N}-c\) for any positive constant \(c\).

It seems likely that \(\upsilon(N)=\sqrt{N}\). I derive a lower bound \(b\) for both \(\tau\) and \(\upsilon\) here, the most significant properties of which are that \(b(N)\) diverges and \(b(N)=o(\sqrt{N})\). Consequently, for \(p'> b(N)\), \(1-\frac{1}{p}\to 1\); so if \(\upsilon(N)\neq\sqrt{N}\), then there must exist some unbounded function \(P(N)\) such that \(P(N\) is prime and \(R_{P(N)}(N)\) approaches some nonnegative number below \(1\).

Remark. My reasoning for imposing an upper bound of \(\sqrt{N}\) on \(\tau\) and \(\upsilon\) is as follows. Suppose \(\tau(N)\) and \(\upsilon(N)\) were not bounded from above by \(\sqrt{N}\). Then one might consider the case in which \(\tau(N):=N^{\exp(-\gamma)}\), as it implies: \[ \prod_{p'=2}^{\tau(N)}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right) \sim \frac{\exp(-\gamma)}{\exp(-\gamma)\ln(N)} \sim \prod_{p'=2}^{\sqrt{N}}R_p(N); \] and since \(R_p(N) = 1\) for \(p > \sqrt{N}\), \(\tau(N):=N^{\exp(-\gamma)}\) satisfies \(\prod_{p'=2}^{\tau(N)}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)\sim\prod_{p'=2}^{\tau(N)}R_p(N)\).

A more general form of Mertens' third theorem can be extrapolated using the identity: \[ \prod_{p'> h}^{x}\left(1-\frac{h}{p}\right) \sim C \prod_{p'=2}^{x}\left(1-\frac{1}{p}\right)^h, \] where \(C\) and \(h\) are constants. It follows that \[ \prod_{p'> h}^{x}\left(1-\frac{h}{p}\right) \sim \frac{C e^{-h\gamma}}{\left(\ln{x}\right)^h}, \] which is relevant to the prime k-tuple conjecture.
